01
The core problem

Six pain points
breaking the system

The government did its part at surgery. What fails afterward is a cascade of interlinked gaps — logistics, finance, awareness, and infrastructure — that together create silence where there should be sound.

1
Critical — affects all 1,700+ beneficiaries
The upgrade gap no one planned for

When the Kerala government launched SruthiTharangam in 2012, the plan covered surgery. But cochlear implant processors have a lifespan of 7–10 years and manufacturers discontinue models after that window. The entire first cohort of ~733 children implanted between 2012–2017 has now aged into a replacement cycle with no systematic government support, no local spare parts, and families whose annual incomes are below ₹2 lakh facing replacement costs of ₹3–7 lakh.

₹34.18 crore invested in surgery · ₹59.47 lakh allocated for processor upgrades for 25 kids
2
Logistics failure
No local dealer.
All parts from Bengaluru.

MED-EL, Cochlear Limited, and Advanced Bionics have no offices in Kerala. A broken cable or a faulty coil — parts costing under ₹5,000 — must be sourced through Bengaluru, taking 2–6 weeks. In Kerala's humid climate, moisture damage is the single most common failure mode.

Avg repair turnaround: 3–6 weeks vs. 1–2 days with local stock
3
Financial impossibility
Maintenance cost exceeds
family income.

Eligible families earn below ₹2 lakh per year. Annual maintenance — batteries, therapy, mapping sessions, travel, repairs — costs ₹50,000 to ₹2.75 lakh. A processor upgrade every 7–10 years adds ₹3–7 lakh. No scheme, no NGO, and no hospital currently bridges this gap systematically.

Annual family income cap: ₹2L · Min. annual device cost: ₹50,000
4
Bureaucratic delay
Scheme transfer froze
approvals for 6+ months.

In 2023, SruthiTharangam was transferred from KSSM to the State Health Agency under NHM. During the transition, upgrade approvals halted. 50 children under age 3 waited 6 months for new surgery approvals. 360+ children waited for maintenance support.

6-month approval freeze · 360+ children affected · CIACS filed protest
5
No tracking system
Nobody knows who
needs help next.

There is no centralised database that tracks which child received which implant model, in which year, from which manufacturer — and therefore no alert system that flags when a child's processor is approaching end-of-life. Upgrades are reactive rather than proactive.

Zero proactive tracking · Reactive-only system · 100% preventable with tech
6
Therapy discontinuation
Hearing the sound
isn't the same as understanding it.

A cochlear implant delivers audio signals — but without ongoing Auditory Verbal Therapy (AVT), the brain does not learn to interpret them. AVT costs ₹25,000–₹1 lakh per year. After exceeding the government's formal support window, therapy usually stops entirely.

AVT cost: ₹25k–₹1L / year · Coverage after age 5: near zero

03
Neuroscience of delay

What silence does
to a developing brain

This is not a device malfunction. It is a neurological event. When a processor fails, the auditory cortex begins to reorganise. Neural pathways built over years of therapy are pruned. Vision takes over the cortex real estate that hearing once occupied. This process is not fully reversible.

"We don't actually hear with our ears — we hear with our brain. The ears are the doorways for sound. If there is no sound information getting to the brain, those auditory neural branches will be pruned away."

— Centre for Hearing, India · Neurodevelopmental research brief
Domain 01
🧠
Cognitive decline

Auditory areas of the brain that go unstimulated begin to lose function — affecting speech recognition, working memory, and concentration. For a student in Class X, this is a direct hit to board exam preparation.

Domain 02
🤝
Social withdrawal

Hearing loss is invisible. Teachers and peers read withdrawal as apathy or attitude. Group activities become painful. A child who socialised normally for years reverts to isolation within weeks of device failure.

Domain 03
💙
Emotional regression

Anger, frustration, depression, and low self-esteem are well-documented outcomes of untreated hearing loss. For a teenager who knows exactly what they are missing — unlike a pre-verbal infant — the psychological impact is acute.

Domain 04
🏃
Physical disengagement

Physical activity for children is mostly social and group-based. Without hearing, the motivation to participate drops. This adds physical health decline to an already compounding set of developmental setbacks.

Domain 05
📚
Behavioural and academic failure

Inability to follow directions and express oneself in class manifests as either disruptive behaviour or total withdrawal. Both lead to academic failure. For the 15–25 cohort, this means missed exams, lost college years, and closed job doors.

Key finding — brain plasticity research

After 3½ years of age, the brain has considerably less flexibility to develop auditory processing skills. But even established pathways degrade rapidly when input stops.

This means every child who received surgery at age 0–3 and developed normal speech — and whose device later fails at age 12, 15, or 20 — is at risk of losing the very capabilities that the ₹34 crore government investment was designed to build. The investment doesn't compound. It evaporates. Silently.

For Private Corporate Companies — the ask

Three ways to
restore hearing
this year.

The government built the foundation. Hospitals implanted the devices. Families kept their children in therapy. Now the system needs a last-mile partner — and that's where IT companies fit precisely.

Action 01
Sponsor processor upgrades via NISH
The government has a formal sponsorship route: pay the implant/upgrade cost through NISH, which handles approvals and procurement. ₹5–7 lakh per child restores hearing for a decade. 10 sponsors = 10 children back in the classroom.
Action 02
Fund a local parts depot in Kerala
A pooled fund across 3–4 IT companies could establish a spare parts inventory at an empanelled hospital in Thiruvananthapuram or Kochi — cutting repair turnaround from 3–6 weeks to 1–2 days.
Action 03
Build the tracking system — zero cash CSR
No centralised database exists. A 10-person volunteer team could build a registry in one quarter: which child, which model, which year — with automated upgrade alerts.
